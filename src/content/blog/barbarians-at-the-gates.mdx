## The Original Barbarians

In 1988, KKR bought RJR Nabisco for $25 billion. They didn't know how to run a tobacco-and-food conglomerate. They didn't need to.

What made the deal possible wasn't operational expertise. It was a new class of financial instrument. Michael Milken's junk bond network gave firms like KKR access to capital structures that turned $15 million in equity into multi-billion-dollar acquisitions. The leverage ratio was absurd. The playbook was simple: find an undervalued company, load it with debt, extract value.

Most of those firms weren't better operators. Many failed. But enough succeeded to permanently change how corporate America thought about defensibility. The moat wasn't ownership anymore. It was whether you could actually run the business once you had it.

## The New Leverage

AI development tools (Lovable, Cursor, Claude, v0) are doing to software what junk bonds did to acquisitions. They're not making everyone a software engineer. They're giving people who understand business logic the ability to build without one.

That distinction matters. The bottleneck in most SaaS platforms was never the individual transaction logic. It's straightforward. The complexity is in encoding thousands of business rules, edge cases, and operational workflows into code. That required teams of engineers who often didn't fully understand the domain, translating requirements from people who did.

Now the people who understand the business logic can write the rules themselves. Not all of them. Not perfectly. But enough to get 70% of the way to a working platform in days instead of months.

And that changes the math on whether to attempt the other 30%.

## What Still Holds

Distribution. Sales teams. Network effects. Regulatory compliance. Data moats built on years of customer activity. DevOps at scale. None of these get easier because someone can prompt their way to a working prototype.

The barbarians of the 1980s still had to operate the companies they bought. Many couldn't. Building the platform is becoming the easy part. Running it, selling it, and scaling it still require everything they always did.

## The Shift

KKR didn't win every deal. They didn't need to. They changed the game by making it possible to *try*. Incumbents who had coasted on the sheer difficulty of replication suddenly had to defend on merit.

AI tools won't topple every SaaS company. But they're about to increase the number of credible attackers, lower the cost of each attempt, and expose which systems were only defensible because they were expensive to rebuild.
